Essay • Civics

On Society

Published February 1, 2024 • 12 min read

Modern societies rarely sit at ideological extremes. They oscillate along a spectrum that toggles between rational, evidence-led commitments and emotion-driven traditions. The tension generates cohesion, bias, and new grey zones in equal measure.

Rationality as a recent experiment

The Enlightenment convinced many of us that evidence should anchor policy, yet this is a young development. For most of human history, communal survival leaned on affective bonds: ritual, religion, myth. These structures provided resilience in scarcity, even if they hardened into hierarchies.

When material security increases—through wealth redistribution, access to education, or civic stability—societies often gravitate toward more rationalist frameworks. But the emotional architectures do not vanish; they recede, waiting to be summoned when uncertainty returns.

Left and right as co-constitutive

It is tempting to paint "left" and "right" as binaries, yet they are mutually reinforcing tendencies. Cosmopolitan cities such as Seoul or New York lean on secular pluralism and personal agency, while rural or peri-urban regions often rely on thick kinship networks, religious institutions, and honour cultures to maintain order.

Concepts like ghairat, izzat, and chodrahat illustrate how emotional economies distribute power. These languages of respect and obligation can nurture accountability, but they can also suppress dissent or innovation. Meanwhile, rationalist frameworks risk hollowing out community bonds in pursuit of procedural fairness.

Governance, culture, and the grey zones

Politically, left-leaning ecologies centre equality and equity. They aim to dissolve discrimination and expand the circle of legal protection. Right-leaning ecologies defend the in-group as a matter of survival, sometimes at the cost of marginalising outsiders. Each approach carries its own vulnerabilities: bureaucratic paralysis on one side, exclusionary zeal on the other.

Cultural disputes over climate action, reproductive justice, and bioethics often reveal the same split. Rationalist responses cite data; traditionalist responses invoke duty, cosmology, or divine order. The grey zone emerges in practice, where citizens navigate both sets of expectations daily.

Mental health as a diagnostic

Mental health reveals how societies metabolise vulnerability. In rationalist contexts, therapy and diagnosis help reduce stigma. In honour-based contexts, the fear of tarnishing family standing can keep distress hidden. Neither approach is fully sufficient: technocratic care can become impersonal, while tradition-driven care may demand silence.

Toward a dialectical reading

The persistence of "right" formations implies adaptive value. They offer solidarity, myth, and continuity. The rise of rationalist systems brings scrutiny, mobility, and reform. Progress may lie in seating both at the same table, acknowledging that human flourishing requires logic and feeling to remain in conversation, not at war.
