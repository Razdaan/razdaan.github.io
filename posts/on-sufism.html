On Sufism

Published August 8, 2023 • 9 min read

Sufism is often cast as Islam's wild, ecstatic counterpart—whirling dervishes, verses of longing, and the dissolving of self. The stereotype misses the discipline. What if Sufism is not a departure from orthodoxy, but its most rigorous interior form?

Essence over form

Orthodoxy seeks correctness: the right rituals, sanctioned readings, the inherited choreography of faith. The danger is fossilisation. Sufism interrogates the form so that the essence can breathe. It strips away superstition, performative piety, and intellectual vanity in pursuit of intimacy with the Divine.

In that sense, Sufism is hyper-orthodox. It insists that every practice must re-route through sincerity. The aim is not rebellion but purification—an attempt to inhabit revelation fully, not merely cite it.

Tawhid as lived reality

The doctrine of tawhid (oneness) is taught in classrooms as theology, but Sufis demand it as experience. Through zikr, music, silent vigils, and the guidance of teachers, seekers attempt to internalise unity until the distinction between watcher and watched thins. The path is ascetic, poetic, and relentless.

Beyond intermediaries

Abrahamic traditions often rely on prophets and clergy as mediators of grace. Sufism honours them, yet it refuses to stop there. The emphasis is on cultivating a direct, unmediated relationship with the Divine—one that holds orthodoxy to its own promise. Liberation is not licence; it is accountability to the heart of the faith.

A discipline of unveiling

At its most demanding, Sufism is a methodology of unveiling. It asks the seeker to submit ego, examine attachments, and become a living testament to the scripture's transformative power. Mysticism here is not soft romance; it is a steady chiselling away of whatever separates the believer from the Beloved.

Seen from this angle, Sufism may indeed be the highest form of orthodoxy because it refuses to let orthodoxy ossify. It insists that faith remain a verb.
