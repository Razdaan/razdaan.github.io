Ethics of Ambiguity Revisited

Published August 14, 2025 • 24 min read

Simone de Beauvoir wrote The Ethics of Ambiguity in the aftermath of war, wrestling with freedom not as abstraction but as responsibility to others. Revisiting her text in an algorithmic present, we find that ambiguity has multiplied. Code mediates choice. Platforms modulate visibility. Ambiguity becomes infrastructural.

Ambiguity as Resource

Rather than treating ambiguity as a problem to be solved, we can see it as a resource—a thickness of meaning that keeps our actions accountable. Each choice holds multiple consequences, each relation carries asymmetry. Ethics begins when we stay with that complexity.

In the classroom, I invite students to practice "delayed clarity." We draft a position, leave it unattended for three days, then revisit it with new citations. The delay allows ambiguity to surface the relational stakes we might have ignored in haste.

Algorithmic Filters

Algorithmic curation narrows ambiguity by presenting us with personalized certainty. Ethics, then, is a counter-practice: a deliberate move toward the unfamiliar. We can design our media diets like we design syllabi—ensuring we encounter voices that unsettle us.

This blog's monologue section is one such design. Each piece stages a tension within the self, holding disagreement as generative friction.

Shared Ambiguity

Ambiguity need not isolate. When we admit what we do not know, we open space for collective sense-making. Community salons begin with a single question: "What is unresolved for you this week?" The answers form a map of shared ambiguity, a guide to where solidarity is needed most.

To revisit Beauvoir is to remember that ethics is not a doctrine but a choreography of care. We keep moving, adjusting, responding. Ambiguity remains the music that keeps the dance honest.
