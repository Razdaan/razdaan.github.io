On Technological Historicism

Published October 16, 2023 • 10 min read

Every epoch begins when imagination finds a new material conduit. Hyperconnectivity is not merely a technical milestone; it is a turning point in how consciousness experiences speed, density, and proximity.

Spirit in circuitry

Hegel would describe this moment as Spirit becoming aware of itself in a fresh medium. Marx would point to the material relations—the labour, infrastructures, and surplus value—that let code circulate. Both remind us that technology is not neutral. It records and redistributes human desire.

When communication networks shrink latency, intention and effect occur almost simultaneously. The window for reflection narrows. We risk losing the delay that once allowed meaning to steep.

Alienation and possibility

Computation abstracts labour into signals, masking the hands that sustain the system. Yet the same abstraction can liberate us if we reorient it toward shared purpose. Marx's dialectic of alienation and freedom still applies: we must decide whether our tools entrench hierarchies or extend collective agency.

The ambition should not be a frictionless future, but one where acceleration remains accountable to embodiment.

Returning to the earth

Every signal travels through matter—sand turned to silicon, copper drawn from rock, rivers cooling data centres. The dream of disembodied progress is a mirage. The planet continues to mediate every interaction, reminding us that technological transcendence is always tethered to a finite world.

Historicism, then, is an invitation to track how technology reorganises feeling, labour, and ecology simultaneously. Progress matters, but so does the pace of consciousness that must live with it.
