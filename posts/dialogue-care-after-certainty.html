Dialogue

Care After Certainty

with Prof. Len Kawabata • Recorded June 2025

Razdaan: When institutions fail, communities are often told to "care for one another" without support. What does care look like when shared certainties collapse?

Len Kawabata: It looks like improvisation. Communities form micro-infrastructures: meal trains, rotating childcare, mutual aid. Postmodern critique reveals that the "official" structures were always partial. Care after certainty means we acknowledge the patchwork and invest in it deliberately.

Razdaan: Our salons experiment with what we call "tender protocols." Participants co-write practice agreements in real time. They're not universal rules; they're living documents that adapt as the group learns.

Len Kawabata: Tender protocols resist the bureaucratic urge to finalize. They trust people to keep negotiating care. That's the heart of postmodern ethics: the willingness to stay in negotiation.
