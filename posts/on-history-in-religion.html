On History as Part of Religion

Published June 26, 2020 • 16 min read

Strategy has always relied on narrative. Long before modern analytics, communities reached for ancestral stories to frame future action. Realists argue that some instincts remain immune to progress; this essay follows that instinct through early Christianity, Islam, and the modern sciences.

Vacancies in early theology

The centuries after Jesus were anything but serene. A fledgling movement wrestled with internal power struggles, imperial politics, and doctrinal uncertainty. The Council of Nicaea (325 CE) did more than settle metaphysics; it filled a vacuum. Greek philosophical categories supplied language for mystery, lending the Trinity a conceptual scaffolding drawn from an "alien" philosophy.

Islam's early centuries mirrored parts of this arc. Translation projects in Abbasid Baghdad flooded scholarly circles with Hellenistic thought. Theologians such as al-Ghazali laboured to reconcile revelation with imported categories. Violence—assassinations of caliphs, schisms that hardened into sects—revealed how narrative authority often rests on those willing to defend their version of the past.

Reformation and the politics of salvation

By 1500, the Catholic Church had amassed bureaucratic power layered with economic exploitation. Luther's challenge to indulgences exposed how salvation narratives could be monetised. His theses sparked a wave of dissent that linked theology, governance, and class anger. Stories about grace reconfigured European politics.

Scientific inquiry, from Copernicus to Galileo, threatened the same authority. The Church's resistance to heliocentrism cast religion and science as adversaries—a perception that lingers in colonial and postcolonial imaginations. Yet the Golden Age of Islam demonstrates that religious conviction and scientific innovation can thrive together when narratives of curiosity are affirmed rather than suppressed.

The stalled progress of psychological sciences

Physical and biological sciences advanced through shared assumptions of natural law. The social sciences, however, remain entangled in debates over human nature. Modern secular frameworks often assume a godless universe, which can bias interpretations of meaning-making, ideology, and sacrifice.

Thinkers from Freud to Marx rooted ideology in instinct—sex, belonging, economics. William McDougall lamented that neglecting the depth of human nature undermined our ability to stabilize societies. The lingering puzzle is why people die for abstractions. Material explanations falter when confronted with martyrdom or revolution.

Oneness and life satisfaction

Recent research hints at what mystics have long suggested: belief in oneness correlates with well-being. A University of Mannheim study surveying over sixty thousand respondents linked a sense of unity with higher life satisfaction. Perhaps the crisis in social science stems from rejecting metaphysical commitments too quickly.

Religious texts understand this intuitively. Roughly a third of the Bible, Qur'an, and Talmud is narrative—stories of past peoples meant to orient the present. Strategy is impossible without such storytelling. The task now is to wield narrative with intellectual honesty, acknowledging its power without surrendering to manipulation.
