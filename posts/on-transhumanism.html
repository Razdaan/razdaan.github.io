On Transhumanism

Published May 13, 2025 • 11 min read

Transhumanism is often pitched as tomorrow's frontier, yet the project of bending biology to intention began the moment agriculture taught us to re-script the earth. Rosi Braidotti calls us "already posthuman" because every tool extends the species beyond its native limits. If so, the question is no longer when augmentation starts, but how we narrate its present layers responsibly.

"We are already posthuman since the agricultural revolution began." — Rosi Braidotti

Three strata of augmentation

Slavoj Žižek's provocations help me name three overlapping strata where augmentation operates: the social, the individual, and the sub-individual. Socially, we have always engineered the substrate that sustains us—domesticating plants, selecting animals, and scripting infrastructures that loop back to rewrite our bodies through diet, medicine, and labour. Individually, we reach for glasses, prosthetics, stimulants, and rituals that let the organism answer survival with cunning. At the sub-individual level, CRISPR kits, neurochemical interventions, and wetware speculation make explicit what was once an artisanal tinkering with hormones and neurotransmitters.

Grouping these strata clarifies that every upgrade has already been a negotiation between agency and constraint. The novelty is not in enhancement itself but in the precision with which we can now edit the code beneath experience.

Simulated experience and the real

Žižek lingers on the weirdness of experiences delivered straight into the nervous system: induced memories, designer emotions, prosthetic sensations. When the line between the actual and the simulated begins to diffuse, identity is forced to confront its own construction. What does authenticity mean if delight can be injected or grief can be paused? Neither nostalgia nor technophilia resolves the tension. We are left debating which experiences we will allow to script our habits of caring for one another.

Source–sink dilemmas

I picture transhumanism as a negotiation over source and sink mechanisms—the evolving loops that let societies metabolise desire without burning out. Historically, these loops emerged organically: the pursuit of myths, the regulation of neurotransmitters, the reciprocity of communal life. A market-driven augmentation paradigm risks replacing shared source–sink systems with privately curated inputs. The ethical dilemma is not that the new inputs exist, but that they can bypass the slow calibrations that kept desires and obligations in dialogue.

Drawing lines without freezing progress

Rapid developments in gene editing, biocomputing, and neurotech tempt us to seek definitive lines—permissions etched into policy like guardrails. Yet history suggests that strict bans rarely halt experimentation; they delay it while concentrating power in darker corners. The challenge is to distinguish iterative refinement from the kind of rupture that renders our ethical vocabulary obsolete. Francis Fukuyama's caution echoes here: humility toward nonhuman nature must be matched by humility toward our own.

Optimism with teeth

For all my skepticism, I remain optimistic. Humanistic, even Marxian, readings of transhumanism invite us to see augmentation as a commons to be stewarded, not an end-state reserved for the few. Equity is not a sentimental add-on; it determines whether progress amplifies or erodes our collective agency. I have little interest in purely physicalist end times—computronium as utopia—but I do believe we are only beginning to understand the phenomenology of augmented life. Progress that stays in conversation with that mystery feels worth defending.

So disagreement is welcome. The posthuman condition is not a manifesto already written; it is an unfolding dialogue about which futures we are willing to inhabit together.
